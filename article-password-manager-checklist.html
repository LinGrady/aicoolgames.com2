Password Manager Buying Checklist for Normal People

June 18, 20268 min readBy Alex Morgan

A password manager is one of the rare security tools that can make life easier and safer at the same time. But the best choice is not always the one with the longest feature list. It is the one you will actually use on every device, every week, without creating a new way to lock yourself out.

Check the basics first

Look for strong encryption, independent security audits, two-factor authentication, clear privacy policies, and a history of responding transparently to incidents. No tool is magic, but a trustworthy provider should explain how your vault is protected and what the company can and cannot see.

Recovery deserves attention

Many people choose a password manager and forget to ask what happens if they lose the master password, phone, or hardware key. Recovery should be secure enough that strangers cannot break in, but understandable enough that your family or business is not helpless during an emergency.

Device support matters more than bonus features

If you use Windows at work, an iPhone personally, and a tablet at home, the password manager needs to work smoothly across all three. Autofill quality matters. Browser extension quality matters. A secure tool that is annoying to use will eventually be bypassed.

Sharing is a real-life feature

Couples, families, and small teams often need shared logins for streaming, utilities, banking portals, hosting accounts, or insurance. Good sharing lets you grant access without sending passwords through chat messages. Check whether shared vaults are included or locked behind a plan you do not want.

Watch for lock-in

Before committing, confirm that you can export your passwords in a standard format. You may never need to leave, but a tool holding your digital keys should not make leaving mysterious.

My practical checklist

  • Works on every device and browser you use.
  • Supports two-factor authentication.
  • Has a clear emergency or recovery option.
  • Offers secure sharing if you need it.
  • Explains security practices plainly.
  • Allows export if you switch later.

The right password manager is the one that improves your habits. If it helps you stop reusing passwords, store recovery codes safely, and share credentials responsibly, it is doing the job.
