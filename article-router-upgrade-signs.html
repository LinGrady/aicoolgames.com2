When to Upgrade Your Wi-Fi Router and When Not To

June 18, 20268 min readBy Alex Morgan

Slow Wi-Fi does not always mean you need a new router. Sometimes the router is old. Sometimes it is in the wrong place. Sometimes the internet plan is the bottleneck. Sometimes one device in the house is misbehaving. Buying first can waste money.

Test wired speed first

If possible, connect a computer directly to the modem or router with Ethernet and run a speed test. If wired speed is also poor, the problem may be your internet plan, modem, provider, or line quality. A new router cannot fix a slow connection coming into the home.

Placement can change everything

Routers work best in open, central locations. A router hidden behind a TV, inside a cabinet, near thick walls, or on the floor may perform badly even if the hardware is fine. Before upgrading, move it higher and more central if you can.

Upgrade for capacity, not only speed

Modern homes have phones, laptops, TVs, speakers, cameras, game consoles, and smart devices. An old router may struggle with many simultaneous connections. If video calls drop when someone streams, or smart devices constantly disconnect, capacity may matter more than raw speed.

Mesh is for coverage problems

A mesh system can help larger homes or layouts with dead zones. It is not always faster than a strong single router in a small apartment. If the issue is one distant room, mesh may help. If every room is slow, diagnose the internet connection and router placement first.

Security updates matter

If your router no longer receives firmware updates, replacement becomes more important. A router is part of your home security, not just a speed box. Check the manufacturer's support page before deciding to keep old hardware.

Buy for your devices

A router with the newest Wi-Fi standard is useful only if your devices can benefit. If most devices are older, a mid-range router with stable coverage may be a better value than a premium model with features you cannot use yet.

Upgrade when the evidence points to the router: poor coverage after placement fixes, too many device drops, outdated security support, or features your home actually needs. Otherwise, troubleshoot first and spend later.
