Troubleshoot Home Wi-Fi Before Buying a New Router

June 22, 2026By Alex Morgan7 min read

A new router can help, but it is not always the first fix. Slow Wi-Fi can come from placement, old client devices, crowded channels, bad cables, weak internet service, or one room that simply needs better coverage.

Run one wired speed test

Connect a computer directly to the modem or router with Ethernet and test speed. If wired performance is poor, replacing Wi-Fi gear may not solve the real problem. Check your plan, modem, cable, and provider status first.

Move the router before replacing it

A router hidden behind a TV, inside a cabinet, or near thick walls is starting at a disadvantage. Try a higher, more central spot for a week. A free placement change can outperform a rushed upgrade.

Separate coverage from capacity

If only one far room is weak, mesh may help. If every device slows during video calls, capacity or internet service may be the issue. If only one laptop is slow, the device itself may be the limit.

Check old devices

Older phones, laptops, smart TVs, and streaming boxes can make a modern network feel inconsistent. Test with two or three newer devices before blaming the router for every slowdown.

When buying does make sense

Upgrade when your router no longer receives security updates, cannot handle the number of devices in your home, lacks the coverage you need after placement changes, or is clearly below your internet plan's capability.
