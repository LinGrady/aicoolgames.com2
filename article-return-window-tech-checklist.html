Buying Process

Use the Return Window Like a Real Tech Test

June 23, 2026By Alex Morgan7 min read

The first two days with new tech are usually too generous. Everything feels faster because it is clean, the battery is full, and you are still in the fun part of buying something. The useful question is not whether the device feels nice on day one. It is whether it survives your normal week before the return window closes.

I keep a short test note for anything expensive enough to regret: laptops, monitors, phones, routers, and paid work tools. It sounds fussy, but it has saved me from keeping a laptop with an annoying fan, a monitor with bad text clarity, and one app that looked great until I tried exporting my own data.

Put the return deadline on the calendar

Do this before unboxing. Add the last safe return day, then add a reminder three days earlier. If a seller requires original packaging, keep the box and inserts in one place until you are sure. A good return policy is only useful if you remember it while there is still time.

Test your actual routine, not the sales page

For a laptop, open your usual browser tabs, video-call app, documents, charger, external monitor, and any odd little accessory you rely on. For a phone, test the camera indoors, Bluetooth in the car, maps, banking apps, and whatever app usually drains your battery. For a router, test the problem room at the same time of day the old router struggled.

Write down the small annoyances

Small issues are easy to dismiss on day one and hard to ignore after a month. Keyboard flex, fan noise, loose ports, a dim screen, a wobbly stand, heat on your lap, confusing privacy settings, or an app that buries export options all belong in the note. You do not have to return the product for one annoyance, but you should not forget it while deciding.

Check setup friction honestly

A device may be technically good and still be wrong for you. If setup requires three adapters, a subscription you did not plan for, a cloud account you do not trust, or a privacy compromise you would not accept at work, count that as part of the price.

Compare against the old thing before wiping it

Keep the old device or old setup available until the new one proves itself. A side-by-side check often reveals that the upgrade only improved one problem while creating two smaller ones. That does not always mean return it, but it gives you a calmer decision.

Decide before the deadline, not after it

At least three days before the return window ends, make the keep-or-return call. If the only reason to keep it is "I already bought it," that is not a product test. That is sunk cost doing its quiet little speech.
